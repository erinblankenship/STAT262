7  Sampling Distributions and Convergence Concepts

In the previous section, we (mostly) explored the distribution of a function of a single random variable. Now, we we turn our attention to the distribution of a function of a sample of random variables. We will also consider what happens to that distribution as the number of elements in the sample tends to infinity.

7.1 Random Samples

Recall: A (univariate) random variable is defined to be a function from a sample space \(\cal{S}\) into the real numbers, \(\mathbb{R}\). We’ve talked a lot about distributions of random variables (including multivariate random variables), and we’ve used these distributions to find probabilities and other quantities like expected values that can be used to make decisions. Often, we are not interested in the random variable(s) themselves, but instead in functions of the random variable(s), such as the sample mean. Specifically, we can focus on functions of the variables \(X_1,\dots, X_n\) observed in a random sample selected from the population of interest. In such scenarios, we need the probability distribution of these functions of random variables in order to make inferences about population parameters, and, in turn, make decisions.

7.2.1 Sampling from the Normal Distribution: Inference

Why do we care about \(\bar X\) and \(S^2\) so much? Because we often use them to make inferences/decisions. Certain functions (notably \(\bar X\) and \(S^2\)) of samples from normal distributions are very important in statistical analyses.

  • \(S^2\) is used to make inferences about the parameter \(\sigma^2\)

    For example, if \(X_1,\dots, X_n\) are a random sample from a N\((\mu,\sigma^2)\) distribution, then we know \(\frac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2_{n-1}\)

  • \(\bar X\) is used to make inferences about the parameter \(\mu\)

    For example, if \(X_1,\dots, X_n\) are a random sample from a N\((\mu,\sigma^2)\) distribution, then we know \(\frac{\bar X - \mu}{\left ( \sigma/\sqrt{n} \right ) } \sim\) N(0,1)

Why might using \(\frac{\bar X - \mu}{\left ( \sigma/\sqrt{n} \right ) }\) as a basis for inference about \(\mu\) present problems? This was a problem we had to overcome in STAT 102.

7.3 Convergence Concepts

When the distribution of a statistic is unknown, it is often necessary to approximate the distribution of the statistic. Specifically, we’ll be investigating the behavior of certain statistics as the sample size approaches infinity. While an infinite sample size is a theoretical artifact, it can provide us with some useful approximations for the finite sample case, as expressions often become simplified in the limit. We’re going to talk about two types of convergence: convergence in probability and convergence in distribution (there are other kinds).

A sequence of random variables \(X_1, X_2, \dots\) converges in probability to a random variable \(X\) if, for every \(\epsilon> 0\),






While we won’t prove this result, the most famous convergence in probability theorem is called the (weak) law of large numbers.

TipTheorem 4 (Weak Law of Large Numbers)

If \(X_1, X_2,\dots\) are iid with finite mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\), then the sample mean \(\bar X_n = \frac{X_1 + \cdots X_n}{n}\) converges in probability to \(\mu\).

This idea that a sequence of the “same” sample statistic approaches a constant as \(n \rightarrow \infty\) is called consistency. To see why consistency is important, we need a couple of new definitions.

In some scenarios we have multiple sample statistics that can be used to estimate the same parameter. For example, if \(X_1,\dots, X_n \sim\) Poisson(\(\lambda)\) we have two competing estimators for \(\lambda\): \(\bar X\) and \(S^2\) (why?). It turns out that \(\bar X\) is a better estimator than is \(S^2\). To see why, we’ll start by discussing some properties of estimators that will help us decide which are better.

An estimator \(\hat\theta\) is an unbiased estimator of \(\theta\) if: \[ \hbox{E}(\hat\theta) = \theta. \] In addition to being unbiased, a good estimator has small variance. If \(\hat\theta_1\) and \(\hat\theta_2\) are unbiased estimators of \(\theta\), then \(\hat\theta_1\) is a better estimator (or, a more efficient estimator) than \(\hat\theta_2\) if: \[ \hbox{Var}(\hat\theta_1) < \hbox{Var}(\hat\theta_2). \]

Remember that estimators are statistics, and are themselves RVs. As such, they have their own distributions.
