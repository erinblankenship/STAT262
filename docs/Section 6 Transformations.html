6.1 Univariate Transformations

Recall: A (univariate) random variable is defined to be a function from a sample space \(\cal{S}\) into the real numbers, \(\mathbb{R}\). We’ve talked a lot about distributions of random variables (including multivariate random variables), and we’ve used these distributions to find probabilities and other quantities like expected values that can be used to make decisions. Often, we are not interested in the random variable(s) themselves, but instead in functions of the random variable(s), such as the sample mean. In these cases, we need to know the probability distribution for the resulting function in order to make decisions. We can’t construct hypothesis tests or confidence intervals based on a particular function of the random variables unless we know how much underlying variability is present or the shape of the distribution. Let’s start by looking at some examples.

6.2 Bivariate/Multivariate Transformations

Often, we are interested in finding the probability distribution of a function of two or more random variables and/or the joint distribution of functions of multivariate random varibles. For example, suppose two random variables \(X\) and \(Y\) have joint distribution \(f_{XY}(x,y)\). We are sometimes interested in a new bivariate random vector \((U,V)\) defined by \(U=g_1(X,Y)\) and \(V=g_2(X,Y)\), and we want to find either the CDF/pdf/pmf of \(U\) or \(V\), or the joint distribution of \((U,V)\). In such situations, we can extend the methods used in the univariate case to the bivariate (or multivariate) case. These are generally much more complicated, especially in the supports. And they involve (for continuous random variables) a Jacobian that’s a matrix. We’re going to leave these for a future class.

However, this Jacobian technique often isn’t needed for the transformation of interest in the multivariate case. Commonly, we are interested in the distribution of something like \(Y =\)

How is this used?

  • Generating random samples from a particular distribution. Take \(Y\sim\) UNIF(0,1) and let \(F\) be a continuous CDF. Define \(X = F^{-1}(Y)\). Then \[ P(X \leq x) = P(F^{-1}(Y) \leq x) = P(Y \leq F(x)) = F(x) \]
  • Hypothesis testing. Suppose you want to test whether a random variable follows a particular distribution. This is essentially testing H\(_0: F_X = F_0\) versus H\(_a: F_X \neq F_0\). If \(Y_i = F_0(x_i)\) then under H\(_0\), \(Y_i\) are iid UNIF(0,1) and you can test H\(_0: F_Y =\) UNIF(0,1) versus H\(_a: F_Y \neq\) UNIF(0,1).
